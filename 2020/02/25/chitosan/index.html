Introduction

Chitosan is a sugar which is obtained from the hard outer skeleton of shellfish, including crab, lobster, and shrimp. It has many applications in agriculture, medical use, bio-printing and weight loss. This short article will focus on its medical use especially acts as antimicrobial agent.
Antimicrobial agents have been applied in clinic treatment since at least 2000 years ago. With more antibiotics discovered, more antimicrobial agent-resistance bacteria appear also. Figure shows the five mechanisms of action traditionally targeted by antibiotics: 1) disruption of the cell membrane, 2) inhibition of DNA or RNA synthesis, 3) inhibition of protein synthesis, 4) regulation and modification of enzyme which participate in cell wall synthesis, 5) inhibition of folic acid synthesis in the cytoplasm.

The mechanism of chitosan of action against pathogen microorganism

Disrupting the cell membrane/cell wall
The most discussed mechanism refers to the alteration of cell membrane permeability and lysis of the cell wall. The antimicrobial activity of chitosan is based on the presence of the amino groups of the polymer chain. These amino groups can be protonated and give chitosan a positive charge. Based on this, positive charge chitosan can combine with negative charge bacteria cell wall, which seriously alter outer cell membrane. Some research demonstrated that the introduction of extra positive charge to chitosan can increase the antimicrobial activity compared with nature chitosan.
Besides, chitosan may also exhibit antimicrobial activity against fungi. However, the mechanism of chitosan interaction may differ with bacteria. Some chitosan-resistant fungi, this polymer seems to be unable to penetrate with cell membrane and remain outside the cell surface.

Interaction with microbial DNA
Another mechanism consists of the interaction of chitosan hydrolysis products with microbial DNA, thus affecting the protein synthesis by inhibiting mRNA.

Chelation of nutrients by chitosan
The high chelating capacity of chitosan towards various metal ions explain the inhibitory effect of chitosan growth. The lack of metal ions will make bacteria more sensitive to chemicals or certain antimicrobial agents.

Formation of a dense polymer film on the cell surface
High molecular weight chitosan may deposit and form a dense polymer film on the surface of the cell, preventing nutrient and oxygen uptake, which inhibits the growth of aerobic bacteria.

Chitosan interaction with prokaryotic cell

Interaction with gram-positive bacteria
The main difference between gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria is the in their outer cell membrane. The main feature of gram-positive bacteria is the presence of teichoic acids, which can interact with chitosan molecules. There are two types of teichoic acids: wall teichoic acid and lipoteichoic acid. Being positive charged, chitosan can interact with negative charged gram-positive bacteria cell membrane, altering cell wall rigidity, disrupting the cell membrane and eventually entering the cell.

Interaction with gram-negative bacteria
The cell membrane of gram-negative bacteria has more complicated structure consisting of outer membrane, plastic membrane and inner membrane. The interaction occurs among LPS and the amino groups of chitosan. Since porins are known to be responsible for the uptake of nutrients, it is possible that chitosan molecules can block the exchange of nutrients by forming a polymer layer on the surface of the cell, thus leading to bacterial cell death.

Chitosan interaction with eukaryotic cell

Interaction with fungi cells
Many studies show that chitosan can inhibit the bacterial cell growth and even kill them, but for yeast cells, only high concentrations of chitosan have antifungal effect and inhibit yeast cell growth. However, the mechanism of chitosan inhibition id not cleat enough and some proposed theories have been mentioned.

Factors influencing chitosan’s antimicrobial effect

The molecular weight and degree of acetylation
The molecular weight of chitosan has a great influence on its biomedical properties. Moreover, the degree of acetylation strongly influences its antimicrobial properties by increasing its solubility and its positive charge, so biological properties such as antimicrobial activity increase once the degree of acetylation is lower. According to different molecular weight, chitosan is often classified as high molecular weight (HWM), medium molecular weight (MMW) and low molecular weight (LMW). HWM forms a dense layer on the surface of the cell, preventing nutrient uptake, while the smaller molecular weight chitosan seems to penetrate the membrane and even bind to DNA and RNA.

pH effect
The pH will influence the solubility of chitosan and chitosan is soluble in aqueous solutions only at pH value under 7. Besides, many studies show that chitosan’s antimicrobial activity is minimal higher pH conditions and native chitosan showed greater antifungal activity bacteria at a pH value of 4.0 rather 6.0.

Other factors
Besides above factors, the source of chitosan, type of microorganism and chitosan complexes will also influence the effect of antimicrobial activity.
