Lecture topics: protein analysis methods

Demonstrate the understanding of different levels of protein structures

Primary structure

The primary structure of a protein refers to the sequence of amino acids in the polypeptides chain. Primary structure usually can be tested by corresponding genes (ORF, open reading frame). For example, the sequence of amino acids in insulin was discovered by Frederick Sanger, establishing that protein have defining amino acids sequences. Insulin is composed of 51 amino acids in 2 chains. One chain has 31 amino acids, and the other has 20 amino acids.

Secondary structure

Refers to highly regular local sub-structure on the actual polypeptide backbone chain. There are two main types of secondary structure: 1) α-helix, 2)β-strand or β-sheet.

Tertiary structure

Refers to the three-dimensional structure of monomeric and multimeric protein molecules. The α-helix and β-strand will be folded into a compact globular structure, which is driven by the non-specific hydrophobic interaction. The structure is stabilized by different interactions like hydrophobic interaction, hydrogen bond, disulfide bridge, and ionic bond.

Quaternary structure

Three-dimensional structure consisting of aggregation of two or more individual polypeptide chain.

Protein extraction

Principle: disruption of cell membrane, mainly by two ways mechanical disruption and detergent (NP40, SDS), and then release protein from cells. Finally using centrifugation to remove cell debris. Cell debris is insoluble in the bottom of EP tube and whole cell protein lysate can be extracted.

Precaution: aim to maintain protein native structure and preserve its activity

Some key procedures: 1), detergent like NP40 and SDS (break membrane structure); 2) add protease inhibitors (inhibit proteases which digest protein); 3) maintain solution at refrigerated temperatures (slow down proteolysis); 4) use PH-buffered solution (resist the changes of PH)

Protein quantification: Bradford assay

Protein electrophoresis

Principle: process of charged molecules in a medium in response to an electric field.
Diving force of electrophoresis: voltage applied to the system by power supply.
Matrix: agarose/acrylamide
Direction of movement: horizontal and vertical

Western transfer blotting (electroblotting)

Separated proteins in the gel are transferred onto a membrane where they are accessible for immunodetection.
Three steps: 1) SDS-electrophoresis-separate proteins; 2) separated proteins in the gel are transferred onto membrane PVDF (polyvinylidene difluoride); 3) immunodetection

Two-dimensional gel electrophoresis

Separate proteins with greater resolution: first dimension: Ph, second dimension: molecular weight

Immunofluorescence (IF) staining

Aim to examine protein subcellular localization
Principle: antibodies can be used for observation of cellular proteins and detectable label is a fluorescence molecule (link to second antibody)

Co-immunoprecipitation

To test whether protein X interacts with protein Y
Procedures: 1) extract protein lysates; 2) add antibody (anti-X Ab) to protein lysates-aim to immunoprecipitate protein X and its interacting proteins; 3) add beads-aim to collect immunoprecipitated proteins; 4) centrifugation and wash off unbound proteins; 5) WB and use anti-Y Ab to detect proteins

ELSA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay)

Overview the knowledge that taught in college before

Yeast-two-hybrid (Y2H) co-transformation assay

Background: when these two domains are expressed as separated proteins, transcription occurs when BD and AD domains are in close proximity.
Procedures: use some reporter proteins to monitor transcriptional activation like MEL1, HIS3 and Ade2. Thought this feature, proteins interaction can be detected by particular selective medium.

Y2H screen and immunoprecipitation/2D gel/mass spectrometry

Mainly aim to search for binding proteins.
