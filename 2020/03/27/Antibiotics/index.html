brief introduction of antibiotics (10 groups antibiotics)

Penicillins

Another name of this type antimicrobial agents is the beta-lactam, referring to their structural formula containing beta-lactam ring. The penicillin class contains five group of antibiotics: aminopenicillins, antipseudomonal penicillins, beta-lactamase inhibitors, natural penicillins and the penicillinase resistance penicillins. Some common antibiotics in penicillin class include amoxicillin, amoxicillin/clavulanic acid (beta-lactamase inhibitor), ampicillin, dicloxacillin, oxacillin and penicillin V potassium (penicillin VK). Some of them like oxacillin and dicloxacillin have resistance to beta-lactamase enzymes by themselves. Others, for example, amoxicillin or ampicillin have better antimicrobial activity combined with beta-lactamase inhibitor like clavulanic acid, sulbactam or tazobactam.

Tetracyclines

Tetracycline are bored-spectrum against gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria and treat some urinary tract infections (UTIs) or sexually transmitted diseases. Tetracycline inhibitor protein synthesis in the microbial RNA (an important molecule that acts as a messenger for DNA). The tetracycline antibiotics contains drugs such as: demeclocycline, doxycycline and tetracycline.

Cephalosporins

Cephalosporins are a large group of antibiotics which can bind to and block the activity of enzymes which responsible for making peptidoglycan, an important component of the bacterial cell wall. Thus, they have ability to inhibit board-spectrum bacteria growth. There are five generations of cephalosporins. Common medications in this class include: cefaclor, cefdinir, cefotaxime, ceftazidime and ceftriaxone. Besides, the fifth generation cephalosporin known as ceftaroline is active antimicrobial agent which has ability to against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA).

Quinolones

The quinolones, known as fluoroquinolones, are synthetic, bactericidal antibacterial class which are mainly used to treat the difficult-to-treat urinary tract infections. Although quinolones show effective treatment in UTIs, this antibacterial class are used in clinic therapy cautiously only when other options are not effective. The FDA updated warning for quinolones on July, 2018 indicating that the fluoroquinolones have side effect on mental health and low blood sugar adverse reactions. (https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-updates-warnings-fluoroquinolone-antibiotics-risks-mental-health-and-low-blood-sugar-adverse). Common drugs in the fluoroquinolones class include ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin and moxifloxacin.

Lincomycins

The lincomycins class antibiotics have effect on gram-positive bacteria like SA. The main mechanism of lincomycins is the binding to the 50s ribosome subunit to inhibit protein synthesis. Lincomycins cannot penetrate the blood-brain barrier but could penetrate the bone, so apart from some respiratory infection, lincomycins may be used to treat bone and joint infections. Some typical drugs in this class are clindamycin and lincomycin.

Macrolides

Macrolides are a class of antibiotics first found in streptomycetes. They are natural lactones with a large ring consisting 14 to 20 atoms. The mechanism of macrolides is similar with quinolones which can bind to the 50s ribosome subunit leading to inhibition of bacterial protein synthesis. Frequently described macrolides are azithromycin, clarithromycin and erythromycin.

Sulfonamides

Sulfonamides are a group of synthetic medicines that contain the structure of sulfonamide chemical group. Sulfonamide antimicrobials are bacteriostatic only stopping bacteria reproducing not kill them. The mechanism of the bacteriostatic of sulfonamide is the interfere of synthesis of folic acid in bacteria which is essential for nucleic formation. Besides, sulfonamide always combined with trimethoprim to kill bacteria, because trimethoprim acts on a different enzyme in the folic acid synthesis pathway. Some typical sulfonamide antibiotics are sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim, sulfasalazine and sulfsoxazole.

Glycopeptides antibiotics

Members of glycopeptide may be used for treating methicillin-resistant staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) infection. Glycopeptide antibiotics are bactericidal and kill bacteria by inhibiting cell wall synthesis with binding to D-Ala-D-Ala, preventing a transpeptidase from acting on NAM and NAG. The resistance of glycopeptide antibiotics in bacteria mainly depends on the alteration of D-Ala-D-Ala to D-Ala-D-Lac. Vancomycin was the first glycopeptide antibiotic approved. Common drugs name includes vancomycin, oritavancin and telavancin.

Aminoglycosides

Aminoglycosides are group of antibiotics mainly used for the treatment of gram negative bacilli infection. The mechanism of antimicrobial activity is the inhibition of protein synthesis by binding to 30S ribosome subunit. Aminoglycosides are often combined with other antibiotics in clinic treatment because it has narrow margin between the safety and toxic dose. Overuse of this kind of antibiotics can lead to impairment of kidney function and hearing losing. Common drugs name in this group include gentamicin, tobramycin and amikacin.

Carbapenems

Carbapenems are group of beta-lactam antibiotics which are injectable and have wide spectrum of bacteria-killing power to some threat bacterial infections. Thienamycin was first discovered carbapenem in 1976. Carbapenems are notable for their ability to inhibit beta-lactamase enzymes (also called penicillinase). The way of killing bacteria is inhibiting cell wall synthesis by binding to the penicillin-binding proteins and interfering with cell wall formation. The typical drugs in carbapenem group include imipenem, meropenem, doripenem and ertapenem.
