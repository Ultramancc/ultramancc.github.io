Antimicrobial Resistance in Escherichia coli

Divided from human samples, we can get six types of Escherichia, including E. coli, E.blattae, E.fergusonii, E.hermannii, E.vulneris and E.albertii. This article will focus on Antimicrobial Resistance in E. coli as a represent.

INTRODUCTION

Escherichia coli is a bacterium with a special place in the microbiological world since it can cause severe infections in humans and animals but also represents a significant part of the autochthonous microbiota of the different hosts. Multidrug resistance in Escherichia coli has become a worrying issue that is increasingly observed in human but also in veterinary medicine worldwide.

E. coli IN ANIMALS: A PATHOGENIC AND A COMMENSAL BACTERIUM

“Colibacillosis” is a general term for a disease caused by the bacterium E. coli. Actually, E. coli is a versatile microorganism with a number of pathogenic isolates prone to cause intestinal and extra-intestinal infections, while most others are harmless for their host and refer to commensalism. Extra-intestinal pathogenic E. coli (ExPEC) infection can cause human urinary tract infection (UTI), cholecystitis, bacteremia etc. Uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC) and meningitis/sepsis-associated E. coli are two common types of ExPEC. For intestinal infection, diarrheagenic E. coli is one of the leading causes of diarrhea, together with other pathogens such as rotavirus, coronavirus, Cryptosporidium parvum, or a combination of these. For example, enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC) strains bind and colonize the intestinal epithelium through adhesins expressed in the context of fimbriae. ETEC can provide two types of plasmid-mediated enterotoxins, of which heat-liable toxin (LT) and heat-stable toxin (ST). In developing countries, these enterotoxins can cause child diarrhea and traveler diarrhea.

RESISTANCE TO β-LACTAMS

There are numerous genes in E. coli of human and animal origin that confer resistance to β-lactams. β-lactams antibiotic is a kind of broad-spectrum antibiotic including cephalosporins and penicillin.

Clavulanic-Acid Inhibited Class A ESBLs

ESBLs belong mostly to class A of the Ambler classification and group 2be According to functional classification of β-lactamases by Bush and Jacoby (Table 1).

ESBL-producing strains of E. coli are clinically relevant in veterinary medicine since they confer resistance to penicillin, aminopenicillin, and cephalosporins, including the third-generation cephalosporins ceftiofur and cefovecin and the fourth-generation cephalosporin cefquinome, which are approved veterinary drugs. Thus, ESBLs may be the cause of treatment failures and limit the therapeutic options of veterinarians, because they have been identified in increasing numbers in E. coli of food-producing and companion animals worldwide. The dissemination of ESBLs genes among E. coli from animals is mainly driven by horizontal gene transfer. But many studies have tried to figure out whether ESBL-producing E. coli identified in humans might originate from animal reservoirs.

Acquired AmpC Cephalosporinases

Although class A ESBL enzymes are the most common sources of acquired resistance to broad-spectrum cephalosporins in E. coli, class C β-lactamases, also known as AmpC-type enzymes, confer high-level resistance to those antimicrobial agents. The main plasmid-encoded AmpC enzymes are CMY-, DHA-, and ACC-type β-lactamases, with a higher prevalence of CMY-type enzymes worldwide.

Acquired Carbapenemases

Carbapenemases have been rarely identified in animal E. coli. The different carbapenemase genes that have been identified among animals in different countries reflect the type of carbapenemases known to be the most prevalent in human isolates in counties.
