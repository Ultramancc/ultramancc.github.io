An overview of the antimicrobial resistance mechanism of bacteria

Antimicrobial resistance mechanism falls into four main parts: (1) limiting uptake of drugs; (2) modifying drug targets; (3) inactivating a drug; (4) active drug efflux. Because of different structures, gram negative bacteria always use four main mechanism, whereas gram positive bacteria may not limit uptake of drugs (do not have an LPS outer membrane) and do not have capacity for certain types of drug efflux mechanism (refer to the drug efflux pumps later in this manuscript)

limiting drug uptake

As mentioned before, some special structure like LPS outer membrane in gram negative bacteria can provide a stronger defense for drug uptake. This give those bacteria innate resistance to certain groups of large antimicrobial agents.
Some bacteria that do not have cell walls, such as mycoplasma and related species, are therefore intrinsically resistant to all drugs which target cell walls like β-lactams and glycopeptides.
Besides, in those gram-negative bacteria with large outer membrane, drugs usually enter cells through porin channels. Under this circumstance, those bacteria have two main ways to limit drug uptake: (1) a decrease number of porin channels; (2) mutation that changes the selectivity of porin channels.
Another widely seen phenomenon in bacterial colonization is the formation of biofilm by a bacterial community. For pathogenic organism, formation of biofilm protects bacteria from attack by host immune system, plus provides protection from antimicrobial resistance agents.

modification of drug target

For drug that target cell membrane, AMR is complicated. One mechanism of resistance to the β-lactam drugs used almost exclusively by gram positive bacteria is via alternation in the structure or number of PBPs (penicillin-binding protein). PBPs are transpeptidases involved in cell walls. PBPs is a necessary protein of the formation of cell walls. Most β-lactam have β-lactam ring, which can destroy the PBPs and correspondingly inhibit the cell walls formation. Besides, the glycopeptides (e.g. vancomycin) also work by inhibit cell walls synthesis, and lipopeptides (e.g. daptomycin) work by depolarizing membrane. Resistance to vancomycin is mediated by van gene which results in change in the structure of peptidoglycan precursors that cause a decrease in binding with vancomycin. Daptomycin requires the presence of calcium for binding. Mutation in genes (e.g. mprF) change the charge of the cell membrane to positive, inhibiting the combination of calcium.
For drugs that target the ribosomal subunits may occur via ribosomal mutation, ribosomal methylation most commonly involving erm genes, or ribosomal protection. These mechanisms may interfere the ability of drug combination with ribosomal.
For drugs that target nucleic acid synthesis, resistance is via modification in DNA gyrase or topoisomerase. By changing the structure of these significant protein in nucleic acid synthesis, these mutations may decrease or eliminate the ability of the drugs to bind with these components.
For drug that target metabolic pathway, resistance via mutation in particular enzymes. Mutation in these enzymes are always located in or near the active sites, and resulting the decrease of the drugs binding while still allowing the natural substrate combination.

drug inactivation

These are two main ways to inactivate drugs: (1) natural degradation of the drug; (2) transfer of chemical groups to the drugs. For example, β-lactamase is most common group of hydrolyzing enzymes.

β-lactamases

Nowadays the most widely used antimicrobial agents are the group of β-lactamases. The members of drug group all share the same structure which consist of a four-sides β-lactam ring. The main mechanism of resistance to β-lactam present as follow: (1) preventing the interaction between PBPs and drugs, because β-lactamases can destroy the four-sides β-lactam which is the binding site to PBPs; (2) using efflux pumps to extrude the drugs; (3) changing the structure of PBPs which can decrease the ability of drugs binding.

drug efflux

Bacteria possess chromosomally encoded genes for efflux pumps. Most bacteria possess different efflux pumps. There five main families of efflux pumps in bacteria classified by their structure and energy source: (1) the ATP-binding cassette (ABC) family; (2) the multidrug and toxic compound extrusion (MATE) family; (3) the resistance-nodulation-cell division (RND) family; (4) the major facilitator superfamily (MFS).
